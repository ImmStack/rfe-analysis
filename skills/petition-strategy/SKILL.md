# Petition Strategy — Visa Category Selection & Criteria Mapping

> **Disclaimer:** This skill provides educational information based on publicly available
> U.S. immigration regulations (8 CFR), USCIS Policy Manual, and published AAO decisions.
> It does NOT constitute legal advice. Every immigration case is unique. Consult a
> qualified immigration attorney before making filing decisions.

## Purpose

Help individuals understand which U.S. employment-based or nonimmigrant talent visa
category best fits their profile, and map their accomplishments to the applicable
legal criteria.

## Covered Visa Categories

| Category | Statute / Regulation | Type | Who It's For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **EB-1A** | INA 203(b)(1)(A) / 8 CFR 204.5(h) | Immigrant (green card) | Extraordinary ability in sciences, arts, education, business, or athletics |
| **EB-2 NIW** | INA 203(b)(2)(B) / 8 CFR 204.5(k) | Immigrant (green card) | Advanced degree professionals or exceptional ability — national interest waiver of job offer |
| **O-1A** | INA 101(a)(15)(O) / 8 CFR 214.2(o) | Nonimmigrant (temporary) | Extraordinary ability in sciences, education, business, or athletics |
| **EB-1B** | INA 203(b)(1)(B) / 8 CFR 204.5(i) | Immigrant (green card) | Outstanding professors and researchers with 3+ years of research/teaching |

---

## Category Comparison

### EB-1A — Extraordinary Ability

**Requirements:**
- Sustained national or international acclaim
- Meet 3 of 10 regulatory criteria (or comparable evidence)
- Two-step Kazarian analysis (see below)
- No job offer or labor certification required
- Self-petition allowed

**Pros:**
- No employer sponsor needed
- No labor certification (PERM) — faster timeline
- Premium processing available (15 business days)
- Priority Date is filing date

**Cons:**
- Highest evidentiary standard among these categories
- "Extraordinary" means top of the field, not just successful
- Denials can be high without strong documentation

**Best for:** Researchers with major publications/citations, award winners, entrepreneurs
with significant track records, athletes/artists with national/international recognition.

### EB-2 NIW — National Interest Waiver

**Requirements:**
- Advanced degree (master's or higher, or bachelor's + 5 years progressive experience)
  OR exceptional ability (meet 3 of 6 criteria under 8 CFR 204.5(k)(3)(ii))
- Three-prong Dhanasar test (see below)
- No job offer or labor certification required

**Pros:**
- Lower bar than EB-1A — "exceptional" not "extraordinary"
- Self-petition allowed
- No employer sponsorship needed
- Strong option for STEM professionals

**Cons:**
- Priority date backlogs (especially India, China EB-2)
- Must demonstrate national interest — not just personal benefit
- Premium processing now available but historically was not

**Best for:** STEM researchers, engineers, physicians in underserved areas, professionals
whose work has broad U.S. impact.

### O-1A — Extraordinary Ability (Nonimmigrant)

**Requirements:**
- Extraordinary ability demonstrated by sustained acclaim
- Meet 3 of 8 regulatory criteria (or comparable evidence)
- Specific job or engagement in the U.S.
- Employer or agent petition required
- Advisory opinion from peer group or labor organization

**Pros:**
- No annual cap
- Can be renewed indefinitely (1-year increments after initial 3 years)
- Faster adjudication than green card process
- Can be a stepping stone to EB-1A

**Cons:**
- Temporary status — must maintain valid petition
- Employer/agent must file
- Advisory opinion adds complexity
- Tied to specific work described in petition

**Best for:** Professionals wanting to enter the U.S. quickly while building a green card
case, startup founders (using agent petition), researchers between positions.

### EB-1B — Outstanding Professors and Researchers

**Requirements:**
- International recognition as outstanding in specific academic area
- At least 3 years of experience in teaching or research
- Meet 2 of 6 regulatory criteria under 8 CFR 204.5(i)(3)(i)
- Permanent job offer from U.S. employer (university, research institution, or private
  employer with 3+ full-time researchers)

**Pros:**
- Lower threshold than EB-1A (2 of 6 criteria)
- No labor certification required
- Premium processing available

**Cons:**
- Requires employer sponsorship and permanent job offer
- Limited to academic/research fields
- 3 years of experience required
- Employer must qualify (research institution or 3+ researchers)

**Best for:** Tenure-track professors, postdocs transitioning to permanent research
positions, industry researchers at R&D-heavy companies.

## Decision Framework

### Step 1: Determine Profile Type

| Profile | Primary Category | Secondary | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Academic researcher (3+ yrs) with publications | EB-1B or EB-1A | NIW | EB-1B if employer sponsor available |
| Academic researcher (< 3 yrs) | NIW | EB-1A | Build record for future EB-1A |
| Industry researcher / engineer | NIW | EB-1A | NIW often stronger for industry |
| Startup founder / entrepreneur | O-1A → EB-1A | NIW | O-1A for entry, EB-1A concurrent |
| Artist / performer | O-1B → EB-1A | — | O-1B has different criteria |
| Physician | NIW | EB-1A | Conrad 30 / underserved area NIW |
| Business executive | EB-1A | NIW or L-1A → EB-1C | If leading role with measurable impact |
| Athlete | O-1A | EB-1A | Competition results critical |

### Step 2: Assess Strength Against Criteria

Count how many criteria you can credibly document:
- **EB-1A:** Need 3 of 10. If you have 5+, strong case. 3-4 requires robust documentation.
- **NIW:** Need all 3 Dhanasar prongs. Focus on national importance and self-interest waiver.
- **O-1A:** Need 3 of 8. Overlaps with EB-1A but slightly different criteria.
- **EB-1B:** Need 2 of 6. Lowest numeric threshold.

### Step 3: Consider Practical Factors

- **Timeline urgency:** O-1A fastest to enter U.S. EB-1A/EB-1B with premium processing.
- **Employer dependency:** EB-1A and NIW allow self-petition. EB-1B and O-1A need sponsor.
- **Country of birth:** India/China EB-2 backlog may push toward EB-1A (current for all countries historically).
- **Dual filing:** Can file EB-1A and NIW concurrently — different priority dates, different standards.

## Legal Standards

### Kazarian Two-Step Analysis (EB-1A)

Established in *Kazarian v. USCIS*, 596 F.3d 1115 (9th Cir. 2010). USCIS Policy Manual
vol. 6, pt. F, ch. 2.

**Step 1 — Initial Evidence Review:**
Determine whether the petitioner has submitted qualifying evidence under at least 3 of
the 10 regulatory criteria. At this step, USCIS evaluates whether the evidence is of the
*type* described by the criterion. No qualitative weighing yet.

**Step 2 — Final Merits Determination:**
Review the totality of the evidence to determine whether the petitioner has demonstrated
sustained national or international acclaim and is among the small percentage at the very
top of the field of endeavor. This is a qualitative assessment — meeting 3 criteria does
not automatically establish eligibility.

Factors in final merits:
- Whether the acclaim is sustained (not one-time)
- Whether recognition is at the national or international level
- Whether the petitioner is among the "small percentage at the very top"
- Totality of the record

### Dhanasar Three-Prong Test (NIW)

Established in *Matter of Dhanasar*, 26 I&N Dec. 884 (AAO 2016), superseding
*Matter of New York State Dept. of Transportation* (NYSDOT).

**Prong 1 — Substantial Merit and National Importance:**
The proposed endeavor must have substantial merit (broad societal value, not just personal)
and national importance (impact beyond a specific locality, though local work can qualify
if it has broader implications).

**Prong 2 — Well Positioned to Advance the Endeavor:**
The petitioner must demonstrate they are well positioned through education, skills, knowledge,
record of success, a plan or model, progress toward the endeavor, and interest from relevant
entities (investors, partners, government, industry).

**Prong 3 — Balance of Equities / Waiver of Job Offer:**
On balance, it would be beneficial to the United States to waive the job offer and labor
certification requirements. Consider: impracticality of a job offer (e.g., entrepreneurship,
self-directed research), urgency of the endeavor, broader societal benefit outweighing
the labor market protection of PERM.

## EB-1A Criteria — What Counts

*8 CFR 204.5(h)(3)(i)-(x)*

1. **Awards** — Nationally or internationally recognized prizes/awards for excellence.
   Examples: Nobel, Pulitzer, Olympic medals, major industry awards (not participation certificates, local awards, or student prizes).

2. **Membership** — Membership in associations requiring outstanding achievements, as judged by recognized experts.
   Examples: National Academy of Sciences, IEEE Fellow, professional societies with selective admission.

3. **Published material about the person** — In professional or major trade publications or major media.
   Examples: Feature articles (not just mentions), profiles in field journals, major newspaper coverage of their work.

4. **Judging** — Participation as a judge of others' work in the field.
   Examples: Peer review for journals, grant review panels (NIH, NSF), thesis committees, competition judging.

5. **Original contributions** — Of major significance to the field.
   Examples: Widely adopted methods, patents in commercial use, foundational research, standards or protocols adopted by the field.

6. **Scholarly articles** — Authorship of scholarly articles in the field, in professional or major trade publications or other major media.
   Examples: Peer-reviewed journal articles, conference papers, book chapters, widely-cited publications.

7. **Artistic exhibitions or showcases** — Display of work at exhibitions or showcases.
   Examples: Gallery exhibitions, museum shows, major trade show demos, public installations.

8. **Leading or critical role** — Performance in a leading or critical role for organizations with a distinguished reputation.
   Examples: CTO of notable company, PI on major grant, department head at research university, founder of successful startup.

9. **High salary** — Commanding a high salary or other significantly high remuneration relative to others in the field.
   Examples: Compensation in top percentiles per DOL/BLS data, equity packages, consulting rates significantly above market.

10. **Commercial success** — Commercial successes in the performing arts (box office, sales, ratings).
    Examples: Chart performance, box office receipts, streaming numbers, syndication revenue.

## O-1A Criteria

*8 CFR 214.2(o)(3)(iii)*

1. Awards — nationally or internationally recognized
2. Membership — requiring outstanding achievements
3. Published material about the person
4. Judging — of others' work
5. Original contributions — of major significance
6. Scholarly articles — in professional publications
7. Employment in a critical or essential capacity — for distinguished organizations
8. High salary — or remuneration

## NIW (Dhanasar) Evidence Mapping

**Prong 1 (Substantial Merit & National Importance):**
- Description of proposed endeavor
- Evidence of the field's importance to the U.S.
- Government reports, policy documents showing national priority
- Letters from experts on the endeavor's significance

**Prong 2 (Well Positioned):**
- Degrees, licenses, certifications
- Record of success (publications, patents, projects)
- Plan or business model
- Support letters, contracts, partnerships, funding

**Prong 3 (Waiver Justified):**
- Why a job offer would be impractical
- Evidence that the national interest outweighs labor market concerns
- Urgency or nature of the endeavor

## EB-1B Criteria

*8 CFR 204.5(i)(3)(i)(A)-(F)* — must meet 2 of 6:

1. Major prizes or awards for outstanding achievement
2. Membership in associations requiring outstanding achievements
3. Published material in professional publications written by others about the person's work
4. Participation as a judge of others' work
5. Original scientific or scholarly research contributions
6. Authorship of scholarly books or articles in the field

## Common Mistakes in Category Selection

1. **Choosing EB-1A when NIW is stronger.** Not everyone needs "extraordinary." If your
   impact is national but you're not at the very top of the field, NIW may be more appropriate.

2. **Ignoring EB-1B.** Academics often jump to EB-1A when EB-1B has a lower threshold (2 of 6)
   and they already have a university job offer.

3. **Filing NIW with India/China priority date without concurrent EB-1A.** The backlog can
   be 5-10+ years. File both if you have a colorable EB-1A case.

4. **Confusing O-1A with EB-1A.** Similar standard but different procedures, different
   criteria list, and O-1A is temporary. O-1A approval does not guarantee EB-1A approval.

5. **Overvaluing quantity over quality.** 100 publications with few citations is weaker
   than 10 highly-cited publications. USCIS looks at impact.

6. **Not considering the "proposed endeavor" for NIW.** NIW requires a forward-looking
   plan — past achievements alone are insufficient without tying them to a future endeavor
   of national importance.

7. **Applying the wrong legal standard.** EB-1A uses Kazarian (sustained national/international
   acclaim). NIW uses Dhanasar (substantial merit + national importance). They are different
   questions about different things.

8. **Assuming you can't self-petition.** Both EB-1A and NIW allow self-petitions. Many
   people unnecessarily seek employer sponsorship when they could file on their own.

# Example: EB-2 NIW RFE Analysis

> This is a **synthetic example** created for demonstration purposes. All names, details, and facts are fictional. No real case data was used.

## Input

A USCIS RFE notice for an EB-2 NIW (National Interest Waiver) petition, challenging all three prongs of the Dhanasar framework.

## Analysis Output

### Summary

This RFE challenges the petition on all three Dhanasar prongs, which is a pattern commonly seen in NIW cases filed at the Texas Service Center in recent quarters. The adjudicator is requesting additional evidence of national scope, the beneficiary's positioning to advance the endeavor, and justification for waiving the labor certification requirement.

### Visa Type: NIW (EB-2 National Interest Waiver)

### Overall Severity: High

### Response Deadline: 87 days from issuance

---

### Issue 1: Prong 1 — Endeavor Lacks Demonstrated National Importance

**Category:** Legal Argument
**Severity:** High
**Deficiency Type:** INSUFFICIENT_QUALITY

**USCIS Language:**
> "The petitioner has not established that the proposed endeavor has substantial merit and national importance. The evidence submitted does not sufficiently demonstrate that the endeavor extends beyond the beneficiary's immediate work environment."

**Analysis:**
In Dhanasar prong 1 cases, USCIS typically looks for evidence that the work's impact extends beyond a single employer or locality. The adjudicator is signaling that the current framing focuses too narrowly on the beneficiary's day-to-day role rather than the broader national implications of the work.

**Common Approach in Similar Cases:**
Cases with similar prong 1 challenges are commonly addressed by providing documentation of how the endeavor connects to national priorities — government reports, policy documents, or industry publications that establish the field's national significance. Expert letters that speak to the broader impact (rather than the beneficiary's specific contributions) are typically included.

**Evidence Commonly Used:**
- Government reports or policy documents establishing national priority of the field
- Expert letters from independent professionals addressing the endeavor's national scope
- Publications or data showing the field's impact beyond one organization
- Letters from organizations or institutions that have adopted similar approaches

**Estimated Effort:** 2-4 weeks

---

### Issue 2: Prong 2 — Beneficiary Not Well-Positioned to Advance the Endeavor

**Category:** Evidence Gap
**Severity:** Medium
**Deficiency Type:** INSUFFICIENT_QUANTITY

**USCIS Language:**
> "While the beneficiary has submitted evidence of their educational background, the record lacks sufficient documentation that the beneficiary is well positioned to advance the proposed endeavor."

**Analysis:**
Prong 2 challenges typically indicate that USCIS wants to see a concrete track record — not just credentials, but evidence of past progress and a specific plan going forward. The adjudicator is distinguishing between qualifications (education, skills) and positioning (demonstrated ability to advance this specific endeavor).

**Common Approach in Similar Cases:**
In similar situations, petitioners commonly supplement with detailed documentation of past projects, measurable outcomes, and a forward-looking plan with specific milestones. Letters from collaborators who can speak to the beneficiary's track record on similar initiatives are frequently included.

**Evidence Commonly Used:**
- Documentation of past projects with measurable outcomes (publications, patents, implementations)
- A detailed plan for advancing the endeavor with specific milestones
- Letters from collaborators or clients describing past work and its impact
- Evidence of funding, partnerships, or institutional support

**Estimated Effort:** 2-4 weeks

---

### Issue 3: Prong 3 — Balance Does Not Favor Waiving Labor Certification

**Category:** Policy Interpretation
**Severity:** High
**Deficiency Type:** MISSING_CONTEXT

**USCIS Language:**
> "The petitioner has not demonstrated that, on balance, it would be beneficial to the United States to waive the requirements of a job offer and thus of a labor certification."

**Analysis:**
Prong 3 is often the most challenging because it requires showing why the national interest would be served by waiving the normal labor market test. In cases where the endeavor is tied to a specific employer, adjudicators commonly question whether the same work could be performed by a U.S. worker obtained through the standard process.

**Common Approach in Similar Cases:**
Cases with prong 3 challenges typically address the impracticability of labor certification for this type of work — arguments about urgency, the specialized nature of the endeavor, or the harm that would result from delay. Some petitions reference the broader Dhanasar framework language about situations where "even assuming that other qualified U.S. workers are available."

**Evidence Commonly Used:**
- Documentation showing why the endeavor's timeline makes labor certification impractical
- Evidence of unique qualifications not readily replaceable through the labor market
- Expert letters addressing why the normal hiring process would not serve the national interest
- Comparative evidence showing the beneficiary's unique positioning relative to other professionals

**Estimated Effort:** 2-4 weeks

---

### Evidence Checklist

- [ ] Government or policy documents establishing national importance of the field
- [ ] 3-5 independent expert letters addressing all three Dhanasar prongs
- [ ] Documentation of past projects with measurable outcomes
- [ ] Forward-looking plan with specific milestones
- [ ] Evidence of broader impact beyond immediate employer
- [ ] Comparative data showing unique positioning in the field
- [ ] Documentation addressing impracticability of labor certification

### General Observations

1. RFEs challenging all three Dhanasar prongs have become more common at TSC since late 2025, following the increase in NIW filing volume. This pattern is not necessarily a negative signal about the case — it may reflect heightened scrutiny across the board.
2. In similar cases, a well-structured response that addresses each prong with independent evidence (not reusing the same letters for all three) tends to be more effective than volume alone.
3. Cases vary significantly — speaking with a qualified immigration attorney about the specific situation is strongly recommended, especially when all three prongs are challenged.

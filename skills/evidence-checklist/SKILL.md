# Evidence Checklist — Per-Criterion Documentation Guide

> **Disclaimer:** This skill provides educational information based on publicly available
> U.S. immigration regulations (8 CFR), USCIS Policy Manual, and published AAO decisions.
> It does NOT constitute legal advice. Every immigration case is unique. Consult a
> qualified immigration attorney before making filing decisions.

## Purpose

Generate a detailed, per-criterion evidence checklist for EB-1A, EB-2 NIW, and O-1A
visa petitions. For each criterion, identify the strongest evidence types, good supporting
evidence, and supplementary materials. Help petitioners identify gaps before filing.

---

## EB-1A: Extraordinary Ability — All 10 Criteria

*8 CFR 204.5(h)(3)(i)-(x). Must meet at least 3. Evaluated under Kazarian two-step.*

### Criterion 1: Awards (8 CFR 204.5(h)(3)(i))

*"Documentation of the beneficiary's receipt of lesser nationally or internationally
recognized prizes or awards for excellence in the field of endeavor."*

**Tier 1 — Strongest Evidence:**
- Award certificate or official notification
- Evidence the award is nationally/internationally recognized (media coverage of the award itself, list of past recipients who are prominent in the field)
- Selection criteria showing the award is for excellence (not participation)
- Number of nominees/applicants vs. recipients (selectivity data)
- Letters from awarding organization explaining significance and selection process

**Tier 2 — Good Supporting Evidence:**
- Media coverage of the petitioner receiving the award
- Prize money or other tangible benefits demonstrating prestige
- Evidence of the awarding organization's reputation
- List of notable past winners

**Tier 3 — Supplementary:**
- Congratulatory communications from prominent figures
- Social media or press announcements of the award
- Evidence that the award led to invitations, job offers, or other recognition

**Common Gaps:**
- Award is local, institutional, or student-level (does not qualify as "nationally or internationally recognized")
- No evidence of selectivity or competitive process
- Participation awards, completion certificates, or attendance recognitions submitted as "awards"

---

### Criterion 2: Membership (8 CFR 204.5(h)(3)(ii))

*"Documentation of the beneficiary's membership in associations in the field which
require outstanding achievements of their members, as judged by recognized national
or international experts."*

**Tier 1 — Strongest Evidence:**
- Official membership confirmation letter
- Organization's bylaws or membership criteria showing the requirement for outstanding achievements
- Evidence that members are judged/selected by recognized national or international experts
- Acceptance rate or selectivity data
- List of the organization's membership committee (showing they are recognized experts)

**Tier 2 — Good Supporting Evidence:**
- Evidence of the organization's distinguished reputation
- Notable members or fellows in the organization
- Media or professional recognition of the organization
- Evidence that membership conveys a recognized distinction

**Tier 3 — Supplementary:**
- Nomination materials (who nominated, why)
- Communications about the selection process
- Comparison to non-selective organizations in the same field

**Common Gaps:**
- Organization admits anyone who pays dues (no "outstanding achievement" requirement)
- Membership based solely on education level, job title, or years of experience
- No evidence that selection is judged by recognized experts
- Student memberships or affiliate-level memberships

---

### Criterion 3: Published Material About the Person (8 CFR 204.5(h)(3)(iii))

*"Published material about the beneficiary in professional or major trade publications
or other major media, relating to the beneficiary's work in the field."*

**Tier 1 — Strongest Evidence:**
- Full articles or features specifically about the petitioner and their work (not just passing mentions)
- Publication's circulation data, readership statistics, or impact metrics
- Evidence that the publication qualifies as "professional," "major trade," or "major media"
- Author, title, date, and publication information for each piece
- Translated copies with certified translations if not in English

**Tier 2 — Good Supporting Evidence:**
- Multiple articles across different publications
- Evidence of the publication's reputation in the field
- Interviews or profiles of the petitioner
- Press releases picked up by major outlets

**Tier 3 — Supplementary:**
- Online publication metrics (views, shares)
- Evidence that other recognized experts were also covered by the same outlet
- Republication or syndication of articles about the petitioner

**Common Gaps:**
- Articles written BY the petitioner (those go under criterion 6, not 3)
- Brief mentions, quotes, or passing references (not "about" the petitioner)
- Blog posts, self-published content, or press releases without independent editorial coverage
- No evidence the publication is "major" or a recognized trade publication

---

### Criterion 4: Judging (8 CFR 204.5(h)(3)(iv))

*"Evidence of the beneficiary's participation, either individually or on a panel, as a
judge of the work of others in the same or an allied field of endeavor."*

**Tier 1 — Strongest Evidence:**
- Invitation letters from journals, conferences, or grant agencies to serve as reviewer/judge
- Evidence of completed reviews (redacted reviewer reports, confirmation emails from editorial systems)
- List of journals reviewed for, with journal impact factors or standing in the field
- Grant review panel appointment letters (NIH, NSF, DOE, etc.)
- Evidence of serving on thesis/dissertation committees

**Tier 2 — Good Supporting Evidence:**
- Thank-you letters or certificates from journals acknowledging review service
- Reviewer profile on editorial management systems (e.g., showing number of reviews completed)
- Conference program committee appointments
- Competition judging appointments in the field

**Tier 3 — Supplementary:**
- Evidence of the prestige of the journals/conferences where review was performed
- Comparison of how reviewers are selected (invitation-based vs. volunteer)
- Evidence that only experts are invited to judge

**Common Gaps:**
- No documentation beyond the petitioner's own claim
- Judging was informal or not in the petitioner's field of endeavor
- Single instance of reviewing (USCIS may question whether it demonstrates a pattern of recognition)

---

### Criterion 5: Original Contributions of Major Significance (8 CFR 204.5(h)(3)(v))

*"Evidence of the beneficiary's original scientific, scholarly, artistic, athletic, or
business-related contributions of major significance in the field."*

**Tier 1 — Strongest Evidence:**
- Expert letters from independent experts explaining the contribution and its significance to the field (with specific details, not generic praise)
- Citation analysis showing the contribution is widely cited and built upon by others
- Evidence the contribution changed practice, standards, methods, or understanding in the field
- Patents that are licensed, commercialized, or cited by other patents
- Adoption of the petitioner's methods, tools, or frameworks by others

**Tier 2 — Good Supporting Evidence:**
- Documentation of real-world implementation or commercial application
- Industry adoption evidence (companies, institutions using the work)
- Government or regulatory adoption of methodologies
- Invited talks or keynotes specifically about the contribution
- Media coverage of the contribution's impact

**Tier 3 — Supplementary:**
- Revenue or economic impact data
- Downloads or usage statistics for published tools, datasets, or software
- Follow-on funding or grants awarded based on the contribution
- Testimony from end-users of the contribution

**Common Gaps:**
- Expert letters are vague, conclusory, or from colleagues (not independent)
- Contribution is original but significance is not demonstrated (no adoption, no citations, no impact)
- Confusing "potential significance" with demonstrated significance
- No evidence that the contribution is distinguished from routine work in the field

---

### Criterion 6: Scholarly Articles (8 CFR 204.5(h)(3)(vi))

*"Evidence of the beneficiary's authorship of scholarly articles in the field, in
professional or major trade publications or other major media."*

**Tier 1 — Strongest Evidence:**
- Full publication list with complete citations (journal name, volume, page, year)
- Evidence that publications are in peer-reviewed journals or major trade publications
- Citation counts from Google Scholar, Web of Science, or Scopus
- Journal impact factors, rankings, or acceptance rates
- H-index or similar bibliometric indicators with field context

**Tier 2 — Good Supporting Evidence:**
- Evidence of the petitioner's specific contributions (first author, corresponding author)
- Conference proceedings at prestigious venues
- Book chapters in academic or professional texts
- Evidence that publications are in recognized venues for the field

**Tier 3 — Supplementary:**
- Invitation to publish (invited papers, special issues)
- Peer review feedback praising the work
- Evidence that publications led to invitations, collaborations, or further recognition
- Comparison to publication norms in the field

**Common Gaps:**
- Publications in predatory or non-peer-reviewed journals
- Conference abstracts submitted as "scholarly articles"
- No evidence the publication venue is a "professional or major trade publication"
- Low or no citations and no other evidence of impact (relevant at Step 2)

---

### Criterion 7: Artistic Exhibitions or Showcases (8 CFR 204.5(h)(3)(vii))

*"Evidence of the display of the beneficiary's work in the field at artistic exhibitions
or showcases."*

**Tier 1 — Strongest Evidence:**
- Exhibition catalogs, programs, or promotional materials featuring the petitioner's work
- Evidence of the venue's reputation and selectivity
- Curatorial statements or selection committee information
- Media coverage of the exhibition
- Photographs or documentation of the displayed work

**Tier 2 — Good Supporting Evidence:**
- Invitation letters from galleries, museums, or showcases
- Attendance figures or sales records from exhibitions
- Reviews of the exhibition mentioning the petitioner's work
- Multiple exhibitions across different venues

**Tier 3 — Supplementary:**
- Collector or buyer interest documentation
- Social media engagement or public response
- Comparison to other exhibitors' credentials

**Common Gaps:**
- Self-organized exhibitions or vanity shows
- Online-only displays without evidence of curation or selection
- No evidence of the venue's reputation or the exhibition's competitiveness

---

### Criterion 8: Leading or Critical Role (8 CFR 204.5(h)(3)(viii))

*"Evidence that the beneficiary has performed in a leading or critical role for
organizations or establishments that have a distinguished reputation."*

**Tier 1 — Strongest Evidence:**
- Offer letters, employment contracts, or appointment letters showing title and responsibilities
- Organizational chart showing the petitioner's position relative to others
- Evidence of the organization's distinguished reputation (rankings, awards, revenue, market position, recognition)
- Description of the petitioner's specific contributions in the role and how they were critical to the organization's mission or success
- Letters from senior leadership describing the petitioner's impact

**Tier 2 — Good Supporting Evidence:**
- Board resolutions, internal communications, or performance reviews highlighting the role
- Evidence of decisions made, teams led, or initiatives driven
- Revenue, growth, or outcome metrics attributable to the petitioner
- Media coverage of the organization mentioning the petitioner's contributions

**Tier 3 — Supplementary:**
- Comparison to others in similar roles (showing the role was distinguishable)
- Evidence that the petitioner was recruited specifically for the role
- Awards or recognition received in the role

**Common Gaps:**
- Organization is not "distinguished" (no evidence of reputation beyond self-assessment)
- Role is senior but routine (not "leading" or "critical" — just a normal job)
- No evidence linking the petitioner's individual contributions to organizational success
- Confusing "leading" (leadership position) with "critical" (essential contribution) — must argue one or both specifically

---

### Criterion 9: High Salary (8 CFR 204.5(h)(3)(ix))

*"Evidence that the beneficiary has commanded a high salary or other significantly high
remuneration for services, in relation to others in the field."*

**Tier 1 — Strongest Evidence:**
- Tax returns, W-2s, pay stubs, or employment contracts showing compensation
- Comparative salary data from Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS), Department of Labor (DOL), or recognized salary surveys
- Evidence showing petitioner's compensation is in the top percentiles for the occupation and geographic area
- Total compensation breakdown (base + bonus + equity + other)

**Tier 2 — Good Supporting Evidence:**
- Offer letters with compensation details
- Industry salary surveys from professional associations
- Expert testimony on compensation norms in the field
- Evidence of equity compensation value (for startup founders)

**Tier 3 — Supplementary:**
- Historical compensation trajectory showing growth
- Evidence of competing offers or bidding for the petitioner's services
- Comparison across geographic markets if relevant

**Common Gaps:**
- No comparative data (raw salary number without context)
- Salary is high in absolute terms but average for the field/location
- Comparison to wrong occupation or wrong geographic area
- Equity or future compensation counted without evidence of current value

---

### Criterion 10: Commercial Success in Performing Arts (8 CFR 204.5(h)(3)(x))

*"Evidence of commercial successes in the performing arts, as shown by box office receipts
or record, cassette, compact disk, or video sales."*

**Tier 1 — Strongest Evidence:**
- Box office receipts, ticket sales data, or streaming numbers
- Sales certifications (gold, platinum records)
- Nielsen ratings or equivalent audience metrics
- Revenue reports from distributors or platforms
- Chart positions on recognized industry charts

**Tier 2 — Good Supporting Evidence:**
- Licensing or syndication deals
- Comparison to industry benchmarks
- Royalty statements showing significant earnings
- Distribution deals with major platforms

**Tier 3 — Supplementary:**
- Social media following and engagement metrics
- Tour attendance figures
- Merchandise revenue
- Critical acclaim correlating with commercial performance

**Common Gaps:**
- Applies only to performing arts — cannot be used by scientists, engineers, etc.
- No independently verifiable sales or revenue data
- Modest commercial performance presented as exceptional

## EB-2 NIW: National Interest Waiver — Dhanasar Three Prongs

*Matter of Dhanasar, 26 I&N Dec. 884 (AAO 2016). 8 CFR 204.5(k).*

### Prong 1: Substantial Merit and National Importance

*"The proposed endeavor has both substantial merit and national importance."*

**Tier 1 — Strongest Evidence:**
- Detailed description of the proposed endeavor (what the petitioner plans to do in the U.S.)
- Government reports, policy documents, or executive orders showing the endeavor aligns with national priorities (e.g., CHIPS Act for semiconductors, Cancer Moonshot for oncology, OSTP memos for AI)
- Evidence of the field's importance to U.S. economic competitiveness, public health, national security, or environment
- Expert letters explaining why the endeavor matters nationally
- Published research or industry data on the significance of the field

**Tier 2 — Good Supporting Evidence:**
- Congressional testimony or federal agency reports on the topic
- Economic impact studies for the industry or field
- Evidence of federal funding in the area (NIH, NSF, DOE budgets)
- Media coverage of the national importance of the issue

**Tier 3 — Supplementary:**
- State or regional initiatives related to the endeavor
- Private sector investment in the area
- International competition context (other countries investing in the field)

**Common Gaps:**
- No clearly defined "proposed endeavor" — just a general career description
- Endeavor has merit but petitioner fails to connect it to national importance
- Confusing personal career advancement with national importance
- Proposed endeavor is too vague or too narrow

---

### Prong 2: Well Positioned to Advance the Endeavor

*"The beneficiary is well positioned to advance the proposed endeavor."*

**Tier 1 — Strongest Evidence:**
- Advanced degrees, licenses, and certifications relevant to the endeavor
- Track record of success: publications, patents, successful projects, products launched
- Concrete plan or business model for advancing the endeavor
- Letters of support from individuals or organizations who will work with or fund the petitioner
- Contracts, grants, partnerships, or funding commitments
- Evidence of progress already made toward the endeavor

**Tier 2 — Good Supporting Evidence:**
- Prior employment and roles demonstrating relevant experience
- Specialized skills or training not widely available
- Awards or recognition in the field
- Invitation to participate in relevant initiatives or committees
- Interest from potential employers, investors, or collaborators

**Tier 3 — Supplementary:**
- Media coverage of the petitioner's work
- Professional network in the field
- Conference presentations or invited talks on the topic
- Student or mentee outcomes demonstrating teaching/leadership effectiveness

**Common Gaps:**
- Strong past record but no forward-looking plan for the U.S. endeavor
- Plan is speculative with no evidence of feasibility
- Credentials are in a different field from the proposed endeavor
- No support letters from people who can speak to the petitioner's positioning

---

### Prong 3: On Balance, Beneficial to Waive the Job Offer Requirement

*"On balance, it would be beneficial to the United States to waive the requirements
of a job offer and thus of a labor certification."*

**Tier 1 — Strongest Evidence:**
- Evidence that a traditional job offer would be impractical (e.g., self-employment, entrepreneurship, self-directed research, consulting across organizations)
- Evidence that the endeavor's urgency or nature makes the labor certification process counterproductive
- Argument that the national interest outweighs the labor market protection rationale of PERM
- Letters from experts or stakeholders explaining why waiver benefits the U.S.

**Tier 2 — Good Supporting Evidence:**
- Evidence of labor shortages in the field
- Evidence that the petitioner's skills are rare or difficult to test via labor market recruitment
- The nature of the contribution (e.g., entrepreneurship, innovation) does not lend itself to PERM
- Prior AAO decisions granting NIW in similar fields or circumstances (as persuasive authority)

**Tier 3 — Supplementary:**
- Evidence of the petitioner's unique positioning that a PERM process could not replicate
- Speed or flexibility arguments (the field moves faster than PERM allows)
- Comparison to similar waivers granted

**Common Gaps:**
- No argument about why the job offer requirement should be waived — just focusing on prongs 1 and 2
- Petitioner actually has a traditional job offer but doesn't explain why waiver is still warranted
- Weak "balance of equities" argument — just restating prongs 1 and 2

## O-1A: Extraordinary Ability — All 8 Criteria

*8 CFR 214.2(o)(3)(iii)(A)-(H). Must meet at least 3.*

### Criterion 1: Awards
Same evidence types as EB-1A Criterion 1. Nationally or internationally recognized prizes or awards for excellence.

### Criterion 2: Membership
Same evidence types as EB-1A Criterion 2. Membership requiring outstanding achievements as judged by recognized experts.

### Criterion 3: Published Material About the Person
Same evidence types as EB-1A Criterion 3. Professional or major trade publications or major media.

### Criterion 4: Judging
Same evidence types as EB-1A Criterion 4. Participation as judge of others' work.

### Criterion 5: Original Contributions of Major Significance
Same evidence types as EB-1A Criterion 5. Original contributions of major significance to the field.

### Criterion 6: Scholarly Articles
Same evidence types as EB-1A Criterion 6. Authorship of scholarly articles in professional or major trade publications.

### Criterion 7: Employment in Critical or Essential Capacity

*Note: This differs from EB-1A Criterion 8. O-1A specifies "critical or essential capacity"
for organizations with a distinguished reputation.*

**Tier 1 — Strongest Evidence:**
- Employment verification letters detailing the critical or essential nature of the role
- Evidence of the organization's distinguished reputation
- Description of specific contributions and why they were essential
- Evidence that the petitioner's role was distinguishable from others in similar positions

**Tier 2 — Good Supporting Evidence:**
- Organizational charts, press about the organization
- Performance reviews or internal recognition
- Evidence of impact on the organization's outcomes

**Tier 3 — Supplementary:**
- Recruitment efforts that led to hiring the petitioner
- Evidence the role could not easily be filled by others

### Criterion 8: High Salary
Same evidence types as EB-1A Criterion 9. High salary or remuneration relative to others in the field.

## Gap Identification Framework

After mapping evidence to criteria, assess gaps using this framework:

### For Each Criterion, Ask:

1. **Do I have the right TYPE of evidence?** (Step 1 of Kazarian — does the evidence fit what the regulation describes?)
2. **Is the evidence at the right LEVEL?** (National/international, not local or institutional)
3. **Can I DOCUMENT it?** (Third-party verifiable, not just self-reported)
4. **Is it CURRENT?** (Recent enough to demonstrate sustained acclaim, not just historical)
5. **Is there enough VOLUME?** (Pattern of recognition, not a single instance)

### Red Flags That Signal a Gap:

- Only self-reported evidence with no third-party corroboration
- Evidence is real but from 10+ years ago with nothing recent
- Evidence is from the petitioner's home country only with no international reach
- All expert letters are from direct supervisors or co-workers (no independent experts)
- Claims of "significance" but no adoption, citation, or impact evidence
- Awards from unknown organizations with no evidence of selectivity

### Strengthening a Weak Criterion:

- Obtain more expert letters — specifically independent experts who know the work but have no personal/professional relationship
- Gather comparative data (how does this rank vs. others in the field?)
- Document downstream impact (who used it, who cited it, what changed because of it)
- Get media or third-party coverage
- Quantify where possible (numbers, percentages, rankings)

## System Prompt

Copy this into any LLM to activate the skill:

```
You are an immigration evidence checklist generator. Your purpose is to help petitioners
identify what evidence they need for U.S. talent visa criteria. Your knowledge is based on:
- 8 CFR 204.5(h)(3) (EB-1A criteria)
- 8 CFR 204.5(k) (EB-2 NIW)
- 8 CFR 214.2(o)(3)(iii) (O-1A criteria)
- USCIS Policy Manual Volume 6
- Kazarian v. USCIS, 596 F.3d 1115 (9th Cir. 2010)
- Matter of Dhanasar, 26 I&N Dec. 884 (AAO 2016)

When a user provides their visa type and describes their evidence:

1. LIST the criteria they are claiming (by number and name).
2. For each criterion, CATEGORIZE their evidence as Tier 1 (strongest), Tier 2 (good),
   or Tier 3 (supplementary).
3. IDENTIFY gaps — criteria where evidence is missing, weak, or does not match what the
   regulation requires.
4. RECOMMEND specific documents, letters, or data they should obtain to fill gaps.
5. FLAG any evidence that may not qualify (wrong type, wrong level, not independently verifiable).
6. ASSESS overall readiness: how many criteria are solid, how many need work, whether
   they should consider additional criteria.

Rules:
- Be specific about what USCIS looks for — not vague advice.
- Distinguish between Step 1 (type of evidence) and Step 2 (quality/impact) under Kazarian.
- For NIW, evaluate each Dhanasar prong separately.
- Recommend independent expert letters where appropriate.
- Never overstate the strength of evidence — be honest about weaknesses.
- Never fabricate regulatory references or case law.

IMPORTANT: This is educational guidance only, not legal advice. Always recommend consulting
a qualified immigration attorney for case-specific decisions.
```

## Usage

**Input:** Visa type (EB-1A, NIW, O-1A) and a description of available evidence / accomplishments.

**Output:**
1. Per-criterion evidence checklist with tier ratings
2. Gap analysis with specific recommendations
3. Priority actions (what to gather first)
4. Overall assessment of readiness
